# The Infinite Costco

## Overview

Nobody remembers when the doors stopped opening. Nobody remembers an "outside." The fluorescent lights buzz eternal. The PA system crackles with announcements for sales that ended centuries ago. The Costco stretches in every direction — far beyond what any warehouse should contain — and the people who live here have long since stopped questioning it.

Welcome to **Costco #4488**, a self-contained megastructure of unknown dimensions. What was once a bulk retail warehouse has become a sprawling post-consumer civilization. Factions war over territory measured in aisle numbers. Currency is the Kirkland Dollar, backed by the unshakeable faith that Kirkland Signature means quality. The Store Manager rules from the back office with an iron fist and a box cutter of terrifying sharpness. Somewhere, deep in the loading dock, a door to the outside is rumored to exist. Nobody who's gone looking has come back.

The fluorescent lights never turn off. The shelves restock themselves at night when no one is watching. The rotisserie chickens are always $4.99, and they are always warm, and nobody asks where the chickens come from.

**Tone**: Played completely straight. This is a world that takes itself deadly seriously. The absurdity is the setting, not the delivery. The DM describes a man willing to die defending his right to a second free sample with the same gravity as a knight defending a holy relic — because to him, it IS a holy relic. Think SCP Foundation containment procedures written about a Costco. Think Dark Souls boss music playing over a fight with a guy on a forklift. The players will bring the chaos. The world simply *is*.

**Scale**: The Costco is impossibly large. Walking from the Food Court to the Tire Center takes a full day. Aisle numbers go into the thousands. The ceiling is visible but unreachable — some say there are things living in the steel rafters. The parking lot is an open wasteland. The loading dock is a mythical threshold. The whole structure follows warehouse logic but at cathedral scale.

## The Quest

### The Membership Card

The party begins at the entrance, where the Greeter bars the way. To enter the Costco proper (and, eventually, to leave it), they need a **Kirkland Premium Membership Card** — not the standard gold card, not the executive card, but the **Platinum Kirkland Card**, which grants access to all areas including the fabled Exit. Only one is known to exist: it belongs to the Store Manager, who wears it on a lanyard around their neck.

### The Arc

**Act 1 — Welcome to Costco** (Areas 1-3)
The party enters the Costco, navigates the entrance gauntlet, and discovers the bizarre civilization within. They learn about the Platinum Card and the Store Manager. They pick up allies, make enemies, and start to understand the factions. Short encounters: the Greeter, the Sample Vendor, the Food Court tribes.

**Act 2 — Going Deeper** (Areas 4-6)
The party pushes into more dangerous territory: the Walk-In Freezer, the Tire Center, the Electronics section. Faction tensions escalate. The party must choose allegiances or play factions against each other. They learn that the Store Manager receives orders from **Corporate** — an entity that may be responsible for the Costco's impossible nature.

**Act 3 — The Back Office** (Areas 7-8)
The party reaches the Loading Dock and the Manager's Office. Confrontation with the Store Manager. The truth about Corporate. The Platinum Card. And the question: if the Exit is real, do they actually want to leave? What's out there? Is there anything out there?

### Possible Endings
1. **Escape**: Use the Platinum Card to open the Exit. Step outside. What's there is up to the DM (sequel hook, void, another Costco, a Sam's Club — the ultimate horror).
2. **Takeover**: Defeat the Store Manager, take the headset, become the new Manager. Rule the Costco.
3. **Burn It Down**: Destroy the headset, sever the connection to Corporate. The lights flicker. The shelves stop restocking. Freedom — and chaos.
4. **Ascend**: Contact Corporate directly. Become regional management. Transcend the store entirely.

## Areas

### 1. The Entrance
**Description:** A vast concrete threshold flanked by shopping cart corrals that stretch into the distance like the walls of a canyon. The automatic doors haven't moved in living memory — they're wedged open exactly 3 feet, enough for single-file entry. A podium stands before the gap, and behind it, the Greeter waits. Beyond the doors, the cavernous interior glows with eternal fluorescence. Shopping carts are everywhere — some repurposed as shelters, barricades, even chariots. A faded banner reads: "WELCOME! MEMBER SINCE ████."

**Hazards:** Shopping cart avalanche (DEX save DC 12 or 1d6 bludgeoning — someone rigged a cart-alanche trap to deter non-members). The automatic doors occasionally twitch and slam shut for 1d4 rounds — anyone caught in them takes 2d6 crushing damage (STR save DC 14 to hold them open).

**Enemies:** Cart Goblins (3-4 scavengers who live in the cart corrals, HP 8, AC 10, ATK +2 1d4 — they throw loose cart wheels and expired coupons. Cowardly. Will flee if confronted by more than 2 people. Will trade information for food.)

**Loot:** A functioning shopping cart (counts as a container — 200 lb capacity, can be used as a battering ram: 1d8 damage on a STR check hit). Expired coupons (worthless? or are they currency somewhere?). A crumpled store map — old, partially wrong, but shows the general layout.

**Connections:** → Food Court (south), → Bulk Foods Aisle (west), → Parking Lot (outside, through the Greeter's checkpoint)

**NPC:** The Greeter (see npcs/greeter.md)

### 2. The Food Court
**Description:** The social and political heart of the Costco. Rows of bolted-down tables and benches stretch across a tiled plaza the size of a football field. The pizza ovens still work — no one knows how. The hot dog combo is still $1.50 and this is the one law that every faction respects. Tribes gather here to trade, negotiate, and fight over the last churros. A massive menu board hangs overhead like scripture, its prices unchanged since the Before Times. The soda fountains flow freely. There are those who worship them.

**Hazards:** Grease fires from the pizza ovens (random event — DEX save DC 11 or 1d6 fire damage). Territorial disputes can erupt into brawls at any moment. The churro supply is finite and people have killed for less. The Berry Smoothie machine produces a liquid that grants visions but also violent diarrhea (WIS save DC 13: success = a cryptic clue about the campaign; failure = disadvantage on all checks for 1 hour).

**Enemies:** Food Court Enforcers (2-3 brawlers who maintain order, HP 15, AC 12, ATK +3 1d6+1 with serving tray shields). They're not hostile unless you start trouble. Starting trouble includes: cutting in line, taking more than one condiment packet, or questioning the $1.50 hot dog price.

**Loot:** Hot dog combo ($1.50 — heals 1d4+1 HP, always available, this is sacred). Pizza slice (heals 1d6 HP, sometimes available). The Forbidden Churro (behind the counter — a legendary churro that grants +1 to all rolls for 24 hours. Stealing it turns the entire Food Court hostile). Soda cup (unlimited refills — the cup is more valuable than the drink).

**Connections:** → The Entrance (north), → Samples Station (west), → Bulk Foods Aisle (south)

### 3. The Samples Station
**Description:** A ring of folding tables arranged in a sacred circle at the intersection of four major aisles. Each table is manned by a Sample Vendor in a hairnet and disposable gloves, offering tiny paper cups and toothpick-speared morsels. The air smells of microwaved sausage and something floral and wrong. Shoppers circle the tables like pilgrims orbiting a shrine. The unspoken rule is absolute: **one sample per customer**. Take two, and you have chosen violence.

**Hazards:** The One Sample Rule — violating it triggers immediate hostility from ALL vendors simultaneously. The "samples" have unpredictable magical effects (see Sample Vendor NPC). A shopper who has been circling the tables for what appears to be years will block paths and mutter about "the good stuff they used to have."

**Enemies:** Feral Vendors (if provoked — see npcs/sample_vendor.md for stats). Sample Addicts (3-4 haggard figures who circle endlessly, HP 10, AC 9, ATK +1 1d4 — they'll try to steal any sample a player is holding and fight for it with feral desperation).

**Loot:** Samples function as consumable potions (see Sample Vendor NPC for the full table). A vendor's hairnet (worn: advantage on saves vs. food-based effects). A toothpick of unusual sharpness (dagger stats, 1d4, but looks ridiculous).

**Connections:** → Food Court (east), → Bulk Foods Aisle (south), → Walk-In Freezer (west, through a heavy plastic curtain)

### 4. Bulk Foods Aisle
**Description:** Towering shelves — thirty feet high, maybe more — stacked with pallets of canned goods, rice bags the size of coffins, and jars of pickles that glow faintly in the fluorescent light. The aisle stretches so far in both directions that the vanishing point is a real navigational concern. Forklifts rumble in the distance like thunder. This is the breadbasket of the Costco — whoever controls the Bulk Foods controls the food supply. Currently contested between the **Membership Aristocracy** and the **Forklift Operators' Union**.

**Hazards:** Falling pallets (random event, DEX save DC 13 or 2d6 bludgeoning). Getting lost — the aisles shift when no one is looking (WIS save DC 12 to navigate, failure adds 1d4 hours to travel). Unstable shelf structures can be toppled intentionally (STR DC 15 to push, 3d6 bludgeoning in a 15-ft line, DEX save DC 14 for half — useful in combat, devastating to infrastructure).

**Enemies:** Shelf Crawlers (things that live on top of the highest shelves — no one has gotten a clear look, HP 18, AC 13, ATK +4 1d6+2, they drop from above with advantage on first attack). Rival faction patrols (stats vary by faction — see Factions section).

**Loot:** Industrial quantities of food (trade goods). A pallet jack (improvised weapon, 1d8 bludgeoning, heavy, two-handed). MRE-style emergency rations (heal 2d4+2, taste like cardboard and regret). Deep in the aisle, a **Kirkland Signature Olive Oil** of unusual quality — the label shimmers. This is one of the **Kirkland Artifacts** (see Key Items). Grants +1 to DEX saves when carried, because everything is smoother with olive oil.

**Connections:** → The Entrance (east), → Food Court (north), → Samples Station (north-west), → Tire Center (south), → Electronics (south-west)

### 5. The Walk-In Freezer
**Description:** Beyond heavy plastic strip curtains lies an arctic hellscape. The Walk-In Freezer was always big, but it's gotten bigger. Much bigger. Frost coats every surface. The air is a knife. Towers of frozen goods form an icy labyrinth — frozen pizzas stacked like bricks, ice cream tubs arranged in defensive walls, bags of frozen shrimp crunching underfoot. Something lives in here. Something large. The temperature is -30°F and dropping the deeper you go. At the very center, encased in ice, is a figure. The **First Employee**. The oldest. The one who was here when the store opened. They might still be alive.

**Hazards:** Extreme cold — take 1d4 cold damage every 15 minutes without protection (warm clothes, fire, etc.). Slippery floors (DEX save DC 11 when running or fighting, failure = prone). Visibility: 20 feet due to frost fog. The deeper zones are colder: damage increases to 1d6, then 1d8.

**Enemies:** Frost Stockers (former employees frozen and reanimated, HP 22, AC 14 (ice armor), ATK +4 1d8+2 cold damage, Special: Frozen Touch — on hit, target's speed reduced by 10 ft for 1 round). The **Freezer Wyrm** (a massive serpentine creature made of frozen hot dogs linked end-to-end, HP 35, AC 13, ATK +5 2d6+3, Special: Cold Breath — 15-ft cone, DEX save DC 14 or 2d8 cold, half on save, Recharge 5-6).

**Loot:** Frozen goods (trade value). Ice Armor (AC 14, but melts in warm areas over 1 hour — ticking clock). The **First Employee's Badge** — if the frozen figure is thawed (fire, patience, or creative application of a rotisserie chicken), they awaken and share knowledge of the Costco's origins. The badge grants access to Employee-Only areas without triggering alarms.

**Connections:** → Samples Station (east, through the curtain), → Loading Dock (south, through a massive freight door — frozen shut, STR DC 16 or fire to thaw)

### 6. The Tire Center
**Description:** A cavernous garage bay reeking of rubber and motor oil. Hydraulic lifts hold vehicles that haven't moved in decades — they've been converted into dwellings, watchtowers, and one improvised throne. This is the domain of the **Forklift Operators' Union**, a blue-collar faction of mechanics, drivers, and anyone who can keep the machines running. The sound of impact wrenches echoes constantly. A tire wall — twelve feet high and remarkably sturdy — encircles the territory. Entry requires either Union membership or a good reason.

**Hazards:** Hydraulic lifts malfunction randomly (DEX save DC 13 or 2d6 bludgeoning if standing under one). Oil slicks (difficult terrain, DEX save DC 10 to avoid falling prone). The Union doesn't tolerate theft — stealing tools triggers an immediate response from 2d4 mechanics with wrenches.

**Enemies:** Hostile only if you cross the Union. Union Mechanics (HP 15, AC 12, ATK +3 1d6+2 with wrench). Forklift Driver (see npcs/forklift_driver.md — can be ally or enemy). If truly hostile, they deploy **The War Forklift** (see Key Items).

**Loot:** Tire Iron (1d6 bludgeoning, can also be used to pry things open — +2 to STR checks for prying). Motor Oil (throwable, 10-ft area becomes difficult terrain, flammable). A functioning car battery (power source — valuable trade good, or can be used to shock enemies: 2d6 lightning on a STR-based melee attack, one use). Access to vehicle repair — if the party acquires a vehicle (shopping cart chariot, forklift, etc.), the Union can upgrade it.

**Connections:** → Bulk Foods Aisle (north), → Electronics (west), → Loading Dock (south, through a service corridor)

---

### 7. Electronics
**Description:** A dimly flickering section where a thousand TV screens play the same demo reel on an endless loop — a nature documentary about salmon that has been running so long the salmon have become a local religion. The shelves hold ancient laptops, cables to forgotten devices, and phones that ring sometimes with no caller. The Electronics section is the domain of the **Kirkland Techpriests**, a faction that worships the PA system as the voice of a god and maintains the store's crumbling infrastructure. Banks of security monitors show feeds from cameras across the store — this is the Costco's intelligence hub.

**Hazards:** Electrical surges (random, DEX save DC 12 or 1d6 lightning damage from sparking displays). The demo reel is hypnotic — WIS save DC 11 when first entering or spend 1d4 rounds staring at salmon. The Techpriests have rigged alarm systems throughout — tripping one alerts them and locks down nearby areas (metal security gates drop, STR DC 16 to force open).

**Enemies:** Techpriest Acolytes (HP 12, AC 11, ATK +3 1d6 lightning — they've rigged tasers from car batteries, Special: can activate/deactivate security gates as an action). Techpriest Archon (HP 25, AC 13, ATK +4 1d8+2 lightning, Special: Overclock — once per combat, all electronics in 30 ft surge, everyone makes DEX save DC 14 or 2d6 lightning). The Salmon — no. The salmon aren't real. They're on the TV. Unless...? (DM's discretion. If a player asks "are the salmon real?" more than once, they might be.)

**Loot:** Walkie-Talkies (pair — allows party communication across areas, huge tactical advantage). A tablet with a partial store map (more accurate than the crumpled one from the Entrance). Battery packs (power 1d4 electronic devices). The **Security Feed** — if the party gains access to the monitors, they can see the Manager's Office layout, the Loading Dock, and the location of all faction patrols currently on camera. This is an enormous strategic advantage. The Techpriests will trade access for a favor: retrieve a server rack from the Loading Dock.

**Connections:** → Bulk Foods Aisle (east), → Tire Center (east), → Manager's Office (south, through a locked security door — Keycard required or DEX DC 18 to pick)

### 8. The Loading Dock
**Description:** The mythical back-of-house. A concrete cathedral of receiving bays, cargo rollers, and towering stacks of pallets that form a maze. The massive bay doors — the ones that theoretically lead *outside* — are sealed. Not locked. Not jammed. *Sealed*, with a material that isn't quite metal and isn't quite organic. It pulses faintly. The air here smells like ozone and cardboard. Forklifts sit idle in rows. A freight elevator leads up to the Manager's Office. This is the threshold. This is as close to the Exit as most people ever get. Scratched into the wall near the sealed doors, in hundreds of different handwritings: "THERE IS NO OUTSIDE."

**Hazards:** Unstable pallet stacks (same as Bulk Foods — DEX save DC 13 or 2d6 bludgeoning). The sealed bay doors radiate a low hum that causes unease — WIS save DC 12 each hour spent here or gain 1 level of Dread (cumulative: -1 to WIS checks per level, 3 levels = hallucinations, 5 levels = catatonia). The freight elevator requires a Manager's keycard or a clever workaround (DEX DC 16 to hotwire, or WIS DC 14 to figure out the call button sequence: UP-UP-DOWN-UP).

**Enemies:** Dock Sentinels (automated pallet jacks that have gained rudimentary intelligence, HP 20, AC 15 (metal), ATK +4 1d8+3, Special: Ram — charge 20 ft in a line, targets make STR save DC 13 or are knocked prone and take an extra 1d6). The Receiver (a middle-management entity that manifests near the sealed doors, HP 30, AC 12, ATK +5 2d6 psychic — it asks questions about your shipping manifest. You don't have a shipping manifest. It doesn't care. If you can produce ANY official-looking document, it lets you pass — Deception DC 14).

**Loot:** The **Server Rack** the Techpriests want (trade good). Heavy-duty packing tape (improvised restraints — STR DC 14 to break free). A **Kirkland Signature Box Cutter** — not THE box cutter, but a lesser version (1d6+1 slashing, +1 to attacks against cardboard-based enemies, of which there are more than you'd think). The **Manager's Keycard** can be found here in a locked office (DEX DC 15 to pick) — grants access to the freight elevator and the Manager's Office.

**Connections:** → Walk-In Freezer (north, through the freight door), → Tire Center (north, through a service corridor), → Manager's Office (up, via freight elevator)

### 9. The Manager's Office
**Description:** The freight elevator opens into a carpeted hallway — the first carpet anyone has seen. It's beige. It's immaculate. Motivational posters line the walls: "TEAMWORK MAKES THE DREAM WORK" (the photo is a stock image of people high-fiving, but their eyes are wrong). "THERE IS NO I IN TEAM" (someone has written "BUT THERE IS ONE IN MANAGER" beneath it). At the end of the hall, a door with a brass nameplate: **STORE MANAGER**. Inside: a desk, a computer that works, a window that looks out onto the sales floor from impossibly high above, and the Manager. They've been expecting you. Their headset crackles with a voice that isn't human.

**Hazards:** Corporate Aura — the Manager's Office is suffused with the will of Corporate. All ability checks made against the Manager are at -2 unless the headset is destroyed or removed. The motivational posters are mildly memetic — staring at one for too long (WIS save DC 13) causes a compulsion to return to work (lose next action going back the way you came). The computer, if accessed (DEX DC 14 to hack), contains employee records, store schematics, and a single email from Corporate that is a string of characters no human language uses — reading it deals 1d6 psychic damage but grants a vision of the Costco from above. It goes on forever.

**Enemies:** The Store Manager (see npcs/manager.md). If the Manager calls for backup: 2d4 Employee Cultists (HP 12, AC 11, ATK +2 1d6, Special: "Clean Up on Aisle [X]" — one cultist per round can call for reinforcements, adding 1 more cultist until the PA system is disabled). The Manager's Personal Security Detail — 2 Loss Prevention Officers (HP 20, AC 14, ATK +4 1d8+2, Special: Detain — on hit, target is grappled, STR DC 14 to escape).

**Loot:** The **Platinum Kirkland Membership Card** (around the Manager's neck). The **Headset of Corporate** (see Key Items). The computer contains the Exit codes — a sequence that unseals the Loading Dock bay doors. A framed Employee of the Month photo — it's the same person in every frame, going back decades. It's the Greeter.

**Connections:** → Loading Dock (down, via freight elevator), → The Exit (through the Loading Dock bay doors, once unsealed)

### 10. The Parking Lot
**Description:** Outside the entrance, technically. An infinite expanse of cracked asphalt under a sky that is always overcast and the exact color of a Costco receipt. Shopping carts drift across the wasteland like tumbleweeds. The light poles still work but illuminate nothing useful. Cars sit in rows — ancient, rusted, locked. Some have been converted into shelters. The edges of the parking lot fade into fog. No one who has walked into the fog has returned. Cart Corrals dot the landscape like ruins of a forgotten civilization. The Cart Narcs — a faction of parking lot survivalists — patrol the perimeter.

**Hazards:** Exposure — the parking lot has weather, which is more than the inside has. Wind (disadvantage on ranged attacks), rain (difficult terrain), and occasional Cart Storms (2d4 shopping carts hurled by wind, DEX save DC 13 or 1d8 bludgeoning). The fog at the edges is a hard boundary — entering it requires a WIS save DC 18 or the character turns around, convinced they've been walking for hours, and ends up back where they started. Getting lost is a real danger (WIS save DC 12 to navigate between landmarks).

**Enemies:** Cart Narcs (HP 14, AC 12, ATK +3 1d6+1, Special: Cart Barrage — push a shopping cart at a target, 20-ft line, DEX save DC 12 or 1d8 bludgeoning and knocked prone). They're not inherently hostile — they enforce parking lot etiquette. Return your cart to the corral and they're allies. Leave a cart loose and they attack on sight. Lot Lizards (actual lizards the size of dogs that live under cars, HP 10, AC 13, ATK +3 1d4+1, pack tactics — advantage when adjacent to an ally).

**Loot:** Scavenged car parts (trade goods). A functioning car horn (usable as a sonic weapon: 1/day, 15-ft cone, WIS save DC 12 or stunned for 1 round). A shopping cart with intact wheels (rare, valuable — most have one bad wheel). In one car's trunk: a **Kirkland Signature Emergency Kit** containing a first aid kit (heals 2d4+2), flares (fire damage 1d6, or signal), and a membership application form that might be useful as a forged document.

**Connections:** → The Entrance (east), → Fog (all other directions — don't)

## Factions

### The Membership Aristocracy
**Territory:** Food Court, portions of Bulk Foods Aisle
**Symbol:** A laminated gold membership card worn as a medallion
**Leader:** **Baroness Costanza** — a severe woman who claims direct lineage from a founding member. Her family's Costco card has been passed down for generations. It's expired, but no one dares say so.
**Philosophy:** The Costco belongs to its MEMBERS. Only cardholders have rights. Everyone else is a guest at best, a trespasser at worst. Membership is hereditary. The $1.50 hot dog combo is proof of divine providence.
**Attitude toward players:** Suspicious. The players don't have membership cards. The Aristocracy will tolerate them if they're useful — running errands, enforcing membership laws, dealing with the Union. But they'll never truly accept outsiders.
**Resources:** Control of the Food Court gives them control of prepared food. They tax trade through their territory. They have the most "civilized" settlement with actual governance, courts, and a surprisingly functional HOA.
**Weakness:** Bureaucratic. Everything requires a form. Decisions take forever. Their military is mostly hired enforcers, not true believers.

### The Forklift Operators' Union
**Territory:** Tire Center, portions of the Loading Dock
**Symbol:** A crossed wrench and forklift key
**Leader:** **Big Hank** — a mountain of a man who has been driving forklifts since before the Closing. He might be the only person alive who remembers the outside. He doesn't talk about it.
**Philosophy:** The workers keep the Costco running. Without the Union, nothing moves. They control logistics, vehicles, and mechanical infrastructure. They want fair treatment, hazard pay (in Kirkland Dollars), and for the Aristocracy to stop treating them like servants.
**Attitude toward players:** Pragmatic. They'll work with anyone who respects labor. Help them with a job, they'll help you. Cross them, and every forklift in the building becomes a weapon.
**Resources:** Forklifts. Vehicles. Mechanical expertise. They can fix anything, build anything, and move anything. They also control a key route to the Loading Dock.
**Weakness:** Small numbers. They're skilled but there aren't many of them. They rely on their machines — without fuel (the Costco has a gas station, and the Union controls it), they're just people with wrenches.

### The Kirkland Techpriests
**Territory:** Electronics
**Symbol:** The Kirkland Signature star, rendered in circuit-board traces
**Leader:** **The Admin** — never seen in person, communicates only through the PA system, which they control. Some say the Admin IS the PA system.
**Philosophy:** The Costco is a living system. Its infrastructure — the lights, the refrigeration, the PA, the registers — is sacred. The Techpriests maintain it. They believe Corporate is a god, the PA system is its voice, and the store's continued functioning is a miracle that must be preserved through ritual maintenance.
**Attitude toward players:** Neutral but transactional. They want the store maintained. They'll trade information (and they have the BEST information — security cameras see everything) for help with infrastructure tasks. They're unsettling but not hostile.
**Resources:** Information. They see almost everything via security cameras. They control the PA system, the alarms, the security gates. They can lock down sections of the store at will. They also have the only working computers.
**Weakness:** Physically weak. Their territory is small and their combat capability is limited. They rely on technology and information as leverage. Take out their cameras and they're blind.

### The Sample Collective
**Territory:** The Samples Station (and they roam)
**Symbol:** A tiny paper cup
**Leader:** None. They have no hierarchy. They have *The Rule*: one sample per customer. This is not a guideline. This is natural law.
**Philosophy:** Generosity within strict boundaries. They offer freely but they WILL enforce the limit. The Sample Collective has existed since the Before Times, and they will exist after. They are patient. They are everywhere. They remember faces.
**Attitude toward players:** Welcoming, initially. Take a sample, enjoy it, move on. Come back tomorrow for another. This is the way. Violate The Rule and they become the most dangerous faction in the store — not because they're strong individually, but because there are ALWAYS more vendors, and they coordinate with terrifying efficiency.
**Resources:** The samples themselves, which have genuine magical effects. They also have an uncanny intelligence network — the vendors are everywhere, they hear everything, and they share information through a gossip chain faster than the Techpriests' cameras.
**Weakness:** The Rule works both ways. They MUST offer a sample to anyone who approaches. This can be exploited by sending waves of people to drain their supply. Also, they're individually weak. It's the numbers that get you.

### The Cart Narcs
**Territory:** The Parking Lot
**Symbol:** A shopping cart wheel
**Leader:** **Agent Sebastian** — a deeply sunburned man in a high-visibility vest who has been enforcing cart return etiquette since before society collapsed. He sees no contradiction in this.
**Philosophy:** Civilization is measured by whether you return your cart. The Parking Lot is lawless except for this one rule: put your cart back. The Cart Narcs are the last line of defense against entropy. Also, they've weaponized shopping carts and they're surprisingly effective.
**Attitude toward players:** Entirely dependent on cart etiquette. Return your cart? You're family. Leave it in a parking space? You're subhuman. There is no middle ground.
**Resources:** Shopping carts (weapons, barricades, transport). Knowledge of the parking lot (which is vast and treacherous). Scavenged car parts. They also know how to navigate the fog — or at least, they claim to.
**Weakness:** Fanaticism. Their obsession with cart etiquette overrides tactical thinking. They can be manipulated by anyone who understands their values. Also, they're exposed — the parking lot has no cover and bad weather.

### The Employee Cultists
**Territory:** Scattered throughout employee-only areas — break rooms, storage closets, the space behind the pharmacy counter
**Symbol:** A nametag reading "HELLO MY NAME IS ________"
**Leader:** The Store Manager (see npcs/manager.md), though most cultists have never met them personally
**Philosophy:** The Store must remain open. The customers must be served. Corporate wills it. The Employee Cultists are the remnants of the original Costco workforce, transformed over generations into a religious order devoted to the continued operation of the store. They stock shelves in the dead of night. They man registers that haven't processed a transaction in decades. They believe the Store Manager speaks directly to God (Corporate) and that obedience to management is the highest virtue.
**Attitude toward players:** Hostile if you're not an employee. They can sometimes be fooled with a stolen nametag or a convincing "I'm the new hire" bluff (Deception DC 13). If they believe you're an employee, they'll put you to work — which is annoying but provides access to employee-only areas.
**Resources:** Numbers. They're everywhere, in the walls, in the back rooms, behind every "EMPLOYEES ONLY" door. They restock the shelves — meaning they control the supply chain. They have the Store Manager's authority and can call in Loss Prevention.
**Weakness:** Blind obedience. They follow orders without question. Forge a memo from the Manager, and they'll do whatever it says. They're also individually weak — it's the collective action and Loss Prevention backup that makes them dangerous.

## Key Items

### The Platinum Kirkland Membership Card
The McGuffin. The only card that grants access to ALL areas of the Costco, including the Exit (if it exists). Worn on a lanyard around the Store Manager's neck. Laminated. Holographic. It hums faintly. The photo on it changes depending on who's looking.

### The Headset of Corporate
The Store Manager's earpiece. Through it, Corporate speaks — a voice that is too calm, uses too many synergies, and knows things it shouldn't. Wearing it allows communication with Corporate. Corporate will offer you a promotion. **Do not accept the promotion.** (Or do. See "Possible Endings.") The headset can also be used to broadcast on the PA system store-wide — useful for commanding Employee Cultists, alerting factions, or rickrolling the entire Costco.

### The Box Cutter of the Manager
The Store Manager's weapon. An ordinary-looking retractable box cutter that cuts through anything — not just cardboard but metal, stone, magical barriers, and the fabric of reasonable game balance. (Stats in manager.md.) If claimed by a player, it's a +2 weapon that deals an extra 1d6 against "packaged" enemies (anything still in its original packaging, which in this setting is a lot of things).

### Kirkland Signature Artifacts
Scattered throughout the store, these are ordinary Kirkland products that have become something more. Each provides a minor magical benefit:
- **Kirkland Olive Oil** (Bulk Foods) — +1 DEX saves, can be poured to create difficult terrain (10 ft)
- **Kirkland Batteries (48-pack)** (Electronics) — power any device for 1d4 days, can be thrown for 1d4 lightning damage
- **Kirkland Vodka (1.75L)** (Walk-In Freezer, half-frozen) — heals 2d6 HP but imposes disadvantage on DEX checks for 1 hour. Also a potent fuel/solvent.
- **Kirkland Toilet Paper (30-pack)** (Loading Dock) — can be used to create barricades (provides half cover), bandages (heal 1d4), or as currency (universally valued)
- **Kirkland Rotisserie Chicken** (Food Court, always warm, always $4.99) — heals 2d4+2 HP. If you ask where the chickens come from, make a WIS save DC 15 or gain 1 level of Dread.

### The Costco Receipt
If the party eventually reaches the Exit, they're given a receipt. It's forty-seven feet long. It contains a record of everything they did in the campaign. Every item taken, every NPC killed, every rule broken. The Greeter checks it on the way out. Whether it's satisfactory determines the final outcome.

## DM Notes

### Tone Management
This campaign only works if the DM plays it completely straight. The world is absurd, but no NPC thinks it's absurd. The Greeter genuinely believes membership is sacred. The Sample Vendor genuinely believes one-per-customer is natural law. The Store Manager genuinely believes Corporate is a higher power. The DM should never wink at the camera. Let the players be the comedy. The world is the straight man.

When players do something unhinged (and they will), react as the world would. If they try to ride a shopping cart into battle, don't say "lol sure" — say "The cart accelerates down Aisle 47, its one bad wheel screaming against the polished concrete. The Shelf Crawler turns toward the noise, mandibles clicking. Roll DEX to maintain control." Make the stupid thing feel EPIC.

### Pacing for Async Play
This campaign is designed for text-based async play. Each area should provide 2-4 sessions of content (where a "session" is a day or two of back-and-forth in a group chat). Key design principles:

- **End on cliffhangers.** When the group goes quiet for the night, make sure the last thing they saw was interesting: a new NPC appearing, a door opening, a distant forklift engine starting.
- **Keep encounters short.** Combat should resolve in 3-5 rounds. Social encounters should present a clear choice within 2-3 exchanges.
- **Let players split the party.** In async, splitting up is actually fine — you can run parallel threads. The Costco's layout supports this with its multiple connected areas.
- **Random events.** When things slow down, use the PA system: "Attention Costco members: [something ominous]." This is your DM tool for injecting energy at any time.

### Player Agency and Chaos
These players WANT to break things. Let them. The Costco is a sandbox — the quest progression (Entrance → deeper areas → Manager's Office) is a suggestion, not a railroad. If they want to immediately form an alliance with the Cart Narcs and lay siege to the Food Court, let them. If they want to unionize the Employee Cultists, let them. If someone wants to seduce the forklift, set a DC and let them roll.

The key: actions have consequences. Every faction responds to what the players do. Burn down the Samples Station? The Sample Collective declares holy war. Ally with the Union? The Aristocracy hires mercenaries. The Costco is a living system and the players are a disruption.

### PA System Announcements (Random Table)
Roll or choose when you need flavor or a plot nudge:
1. "Attention Costco members: the churro machine will be undergoing scheduled maintenance. This is not a drill. We repeat: this is not a drill."
2. "Will the owner of a blue... vehicle... in the parking lot please... [thirty seconds of static] ...thank you."
3. "Cleanup on Aisle 7,000. Cleanup is... significant."
4. "Attention: today's samples include [roll on Sample Table]. Limit one per customer. This policy will be enforced."
5. "The Costco will be closing in fifteen minutes." (It will not be closing. It has never closed. But everyone tenses when they hear it.)
6. "Corporate would like to remind all associates that overtime is mandatory and existence is a privilege."
7. "Lost child in Electronics. Description: approximately forty years old, wearing a lanyard, answers to 'Employee.' If found, do not make eye contact."
8. "The tire center is experiencing a noise complaint. The noise is screaming. The complaint is from the tires."

### Scaling
For 3 players, use the lower end of enemy counts. For 5 players, add 1-2 extra enemies per encounter and increase boss HP by 10. The real challenge should be navigating faction politics, not combat — combat is the punctuation, not the sentence.

### The Big Secret
The Costco is a containment structure. Corporate is an extradimensional entity that feeds on consumer behavior — the browsing, the impulse buys, the loyalty, the membership renewals. The store is a farm. The people inside are the crop. The Store Manager knows this and has made peace with it. The Greeter has always known. The Exit is real, but what's outside isn't the world the players remember. It's another store. It's always been another store.

Or maybe it's a parking lot and a Starbucks and the sun is shining and birds are singing and the whole thing was a fever dream triggered by eating too many samples on a Saturday afternoon.

The DM decides. Both are true until observed.
